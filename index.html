The Anatomy of a Phishing Email

Phishing emails trick users into clicking malicious links, opening dangerous attachments, or giving away private information.

From: support@paypa1-security.com

Subject: URGENT: Verify Your Account Now

Dear Customer,

We detected unusual activity on your account. For your security, verify your account immediately.

Secure verification link:
http://paypal-login-security-check.com

If you do not verify within 24 hours, your account will be suspended.

Thank you,
PayPal Securrity Teamm

Attachment: Account_Verification_Form.zip

1. Fake Sender Address

The domain looks similar to PayPal but contains suspicious spelling.

2. Urgent Subject Line

Attackers create panic so users act without thinking.

3. Suspicious Link

The link does not match the official website.

4. Spelling Mistakes

Poor grammar and misspelled words are common warning signs.

5. Unexpected Attachment

Dangerous files may install malware or steal data.

Red Flags Checklist

  • Unknown or fake sender address
  • Urgent or threatening language
  • Suspicious links or shortened URLs
  • Spelling and grammar errors
  • Unexpected attachments
  • Requests for passwords or personal information

Reporting Protocol

If you receive a suspicious email, follow this step-by-step process immediately.

Do NOT Click

Do not click links, buttons, or attachments in the suspicious email.

Do NOT Reply

Replying confirms your account is active to attackers.

Take Screenshot

Capture evidence before deleting or reporting the message.

Report to IT

Send it to the IT or Security team using the official channel.

Delete Email

Delete it after reporting so nobody opens it later.

Where to Report

Email: security@yourcompany.com

Phone: (555) 123-4567

Portal: security.yourcompany.com/report

Password Hygiene

Strong passwords and MFA protect your accounts even when attackers try to steal login details.

What is a Strong Password?

A strong password is long, unique, and difficult to guess.

Weak: password123

Strong: BlueTiger!Cloud#92

Length > Complexity

  • Use 12 or more characters
  • Use phrases that are easy to remember
  • Avoid personal information
  • Do not reuse passwords

What is MFA?

Multi-Factor Authentication adds another layer of security, such as a phone code, fingerprint, or authenticator app.

Why it matters: even if your password is stolen, MFA can stop attackers.
